# The Feast of Ashes — Research Sources

A curated bibliography of scholarly and primary sources organized by topic. Each entry includes author, title, publication year, and a note on relevance.

---

## Venetian History and Politics

1. **Frederic C. Lane**, *Venice: A Maritime Republic* (1973)
   The definitive English-language history of Venice from its founding through the fall of the Republic; indispensable for political structure, commerce, and the Arsenal.

2. **John Julius Norwich**, *A History of Venice* (1982)
   Readable narrative history covering the full sweep of Venetian history, with strong coverage of the Italian Wars period and Doge Agostino Barbarigo's reign.

3. **Dennis Romano**, *Patricians and Popolani: The Social Foundations of the Venetian Renaissance State* (1987)
   Examines the relationship between Venice's ruling class and its common citizens, including guild structures and social mobility.

4. **Robert Finlay**, *Politics in Renaissance Venice* (1980)
   Detailed analysis of the Great Council, the Council of Ten, and the mechanics of Venetian political decision-making in the 15th and 16th centuries.

5. **Stanley Chojnacki**, *Women and Men in Renaissance Venice* (2000)
   Twelve essays on gender, family, marriage, and social structure among the Venetian patriciate, with extensive archival research.

6. **Joanne M. Ferraro**, *Venice: History of the Floating City* (2012)
   A modern synthesis covering Venetian social, cultural, and political history with attention to everyday life and material culture.

7. **Elisabeth Crouzet-Pavan**, *Venice Triumphant: The Horizons of a Myth* (French 1999; English trans. 2002)
   Explores the physical environment of Venice — water management, urban space, and how the city shaped its inhabitants' lives.

8. **Michael Mallett and Christine Shaw**, *The Italian Wars 1494–1559: War, State and Society in Early Modern Europe* (2012)
   Comprehensive military and political history of the wars that engulfed Italy; essential for understanding the League of Venice, Fornovo, and the diplomatic context.

9. **David Abulafia (ed.)**, *The French Descent into Renaissance Italy, 1494–95: Antecedents and Effects* (1995)
   Collected essays on Charles VIII's invasion, its causes, and its consequences for Italian states including Venice.

10. **Paolo Preto**, *I servizi segreti di Venezia* (1994)
    The definitive study of Venetian espionage, the Council of Ten's intelligence operations, informers, and state surveillance — in Italian but essential for the spy-state atmosphere.

## Murano and Glassmaking

11. **W. Patrick McCray**, *Glassmaking in Renaissance Venice: The Fragile Craft* (1999)
    The best English-language study of Murano glassmaking, covering technology, guild structure, economics, and secrecy laws from the 13th through 16th centuries.

12. **Luigi Zecchin**, *Vetro e vetrai di Murano: Studi sulla storia del vetro*, 3 vols. (1987–1990)
    Exhaustive Italian-language study based on Murano archival records; the primary scholarly source for individual glassmakers, families, and guild regulations.

13. **Rosa Barovier Mentasti**, *Il vetro veneziano dal Medioevo al Novecento* (1982)
    Comprehensive history of Venetian glass with extensive illustrations, covering techniques, styles, and the evolution of the industry.

14. **Francesca Trivellato**, *Fondamenta dei vetrai: Lavoro, tecnologia e mercato a Murano fra Sei e Settecento* (2000)
    Detailed study of Murano labor relations, working conditions, and the economics of glass production, focused on a slightly later period but deeply relevant.

15. **David Whitehouse**, *Medieval Glass for Popes, Princes, and Peasants* (2010)
    Catalog and study of medieval and Renaissance glass objects, with excellent photographs and technical descriptions of Venetian glass types.

16. **Marco Verità**, "Analyses of Early Enamelled Venetian Glass: A Comparison with Islamic Glass," in *Annales du 13e Congrès de l'Association Internationale pour l'Histoire du Verre* (1996)
    Technical analysis of Venetian glass composition, revealing raw materials and recipes used in the 15th century.

## Jewish Life in Venice

17. **Benjamin Ravid**, *Studies on the Jews of Venice, 1382–1797* (2003)
    Collected essays by the leading scholar of Venetian Jewish history, covering the condotta system, the pre-Ghetto period, and Jewish-Christian economic relations.

18. **Robert C. Davis and Benjamin Ravid (eds.)**, *The Jews of Early Modern Venice* (2001)
    Essential essay collection covering Jewish life in Venice across multiple dimensions — legal status, economic activity, cultural production, and community organization.

19. **Riccardo Calimani**, *The Ghetto of Venice* (Italian 1985; English trans. 1987)
    Narrative history of the Venetian Jewish community from the Middle Ages through the fall of the Republic, with attention to daily life and community institutions.

20. **Cecil Roth**, *The History of the Jews of Italy* (1946)
    Classic survey of Italian Jewish history, with substantial coverage of Venice and the Veneto region; still useful for broad context despite its age.

21. **David Malkiel**, *A Separate Republic: The Mechanics and Dynamics of Venetian Jewish Self-Government, 1607–1624* (1991)
    Focused on a later period but illuminating for understanding the structures of Jewish self-governance that existed in embryonic form in the 1490s.

22. **Brian Pullan**, *Rich and Poor in Renaissance Venice: The Social Institutions of a Catholic State, to 1620* (1971)
    Includes substantial discussion of Jewish moneylending, the Monte di Pieta movement, and the economic role Jews played in Venetian society.

23. **Kenneth R. Stow**, *Alienated Minority: The Jews of Medieval Latin Europe* (1992)
    Broad study of Jewish life in medieval Europe with attention to legal status, the badge requirement, occupational restrictions, and community life.

## Renaissance Optics and Science

24. **David C. Lindberg**, *Theories of Vision from al-Kindi to Kepler* (1976)
    The standard history of optical theory from the Arabic tradition through the Renaissance; essential for understanding what was known about lenses and light in the 1490s.

25. **A. Mark Smith**, *From Sight to Light: The Passage from Ancient to Modern Optics* (2015)
    Comprehensive intellectual history of optics from antiquity through the Scientific Revolution, with detailed treatment of Alhazen, Bacon, and the medieval perspectivists.

26. **Vincent Ilardi**, *Renaissance Vision from Spectacles to Telescopes* (2007)
    The definitive study of spectacle-making and lens technology in Renaissance Italy, with extensive coverage of Venice as a production center and detailed discussion of grinding techniques.

27. **Eileen Reeves**, *Galileo's Glassworks: The Telescope and the Mirror* (2008)
    Explores the connections between Venetian glassmaking and optical instrument development, tracing the path from Murano furnaces to Galileo's telescope.

28. **Sven Dupré**, "Trading Luxury Glass, Picturing Collections and Consuming Objects of Knowledge in Early Seventeenth-Century Antwerp," in *Bentkowska-Kafel et al. (eds.), Digital Visual Culture* (2009)
    Examines the interplay between glass technology and optical knowledge in the early modern period.

29. **Roger Bacon**, *Opus Majus*, Part V: *Perspectiva* (c. 1267; various modern editions)
    Primary source — Bacon's own speculations about military uses of lenses and mirrors, including the idea of burning enemies at a distance.

## Daily Life and Material Culture

30. **Patricia Fortini Brown**, *Private Lives in Renaissance Venice: Art, Architecture, and the Family* (2004)
    Richly illustrated study of domestic life among the Venetian upper classes — interiors, furnishings, food, clothing, and family rituals.

31. **Luca Molà**, *The Silk Industry of Renaissance Venice* (2000)
    Detailed economic and social history of one of Venice's major industries, with insights into guild regulation, labor practices, and material culture.

32. **Peter Burke**, *The Historical Anthropology of Early Modern Italy: Essays on Perception and Communication* (1987)
    Wide-ranging essays on Italian daily life, popular culture, festivals, communication, and social rituals, with several chapters on Venice.

33. **Elizabeth Horodowich**, *A Brief History of Venice: A New History of the City and Its People* (2009)
    Accessible social history emphasizing the lived experience of ordinary Venetians — food, work, leisure, crime, and neighborhood life.

34. **Manlio Brusatin**, *Venezia nel Settecento: Stato, architettura, territorio* (1980)
    Though focused on a later period, contains valuable material on the built environment, streetscapes, and architectural spaces of Venice.

35. **Margaret F. Rosenthal**, *The Honest Courtesan: Veronica Franco, Citizen and Writer in Sixteenth-Century Venice* (1992)
    Social history of Venice through the life of a famous courtesan; invaluable for understanding women's lives, street culture, and the texture of daily existence.

36. **Edward Muir**, *Civic Ritual in Renaissance Venice* (1981)
    Detailed study of Venetian public ceremonies, festivals, processions, and rituals — from the Sensa to Carnival — and their political meanings.

## The Italian Wars

37. **Christine Shaw**, *Italy and the European Powers: The Impact of War, 1500–1530* (2006)
    Examines how the Italian Wars transformed Italian states, including Venice's military and diplomatic responses.

38. **Michael Mallett**, *Mercenaries and Their Masters: Warfare in Renaissance Italy* (1974)
    Study of Italian military organization including Venice's use of condottieri, the Stradiots, and the evolving nature of warfare in the period.

39. **M.E. Mallett and J.R. Hale**, *The Military Organisation of a Renaissance State: Venice c. 1400 to 1617* (1984)
    The definitive study of Venice's military system — the Arsenal, the navy, land forces, fortifications, and the bureaucracy that managed them.

## Primary Sources and Contemporary Accounts

40. **Marin Sanudo**, *I Diarii* (1496–1533; published 1879–1903, 58 volumes)
    The monumental diary of a Venetian patrician who recorded daily political, social, and economic events in extraordinary detail; the single most important primary source for late 15th-century Venice.

41. **Philippe de Commynes**, *Memoirs* (c. 1489–1498; various modern editions)
    French diplomat and historian who visited Venice in 1495 and left a vivid eyewitness description of the city, its government, and its response to the Italian Wars.

42. **Pietro Casola**, *Canon Pietro Casola's Pilgrimage to Jerusalem in the Year 1494* (trans. M. Margaret Newett, 1907)
    A Milanese canon's account of traveling through Venice en route to the Holy Land; includes detailed descriptions of the city, its markets, churches, and social life in the exact year the novel begins.

43. **Arnold von Harff**, *The Pilgrimage of Arnold von Harff, Knight, from Cologne* (1496–1499; trans. Malcolm Letts, 1946)
    A German knight's travel account including observations on Venice, its people, customs, and trade in the novel's time period.

44. **Francesco Colonna**, *Hypnerotomachia Poliphili* (1499, printed by Aldus Manutius in Venice)
    An allegorical romance printed in Venice with lavish woodcuts; though fictional, it reflects Venetian aesthetic sensibilities and architectural ideals of the exact period.

45. **Sabellico (Marcantonio Coccio)**, *De situ urbis Venetae* (1494)
    A humanist description of Venice written in the very year the novel begins; describes the city's topography, monuments, and institutions.

## Additional Specialized Sources

46. **Deborah Howard**, *The Architectural History of Venice* (1980; revised 2002)
    Comprehensive guide to Venetian architecture from the Byzantine period through the Baroque, with plans, photographs, and detailed descriptions of individual buildings.

47. **Deborah Howard**, *Venice and the East: The Impact of the Islamic World on Venetian Architecture, 1100–1500* (2000)
    Explores the Islamic influences on Venetian design — arches, decoration, spatial organization — crucial for understanding Venice's hybrid architectural character.

48. **Pompeo Molmenti**, *Venice: Its Individual Growth from the Earliest Beginnings to the Fall of the Republic*, 6 vols. (Italian 1879; English trans. 1906–1908)
    Classic multivolume cultural history covering every aspect of Venetian life — dress, food, entertainment, customs, trade — richly detailed though dated in interpretation.

49. **Alberto Ferrante del Nero**, *Le arti dei vetrai muranesi attraverso i secoli* (1956)
    Specialized study of the Murano glassmakers' guild regulations across several centuries, drawn from archival sources.

50. **Rolf C. Wirtz**, *Art and Architecture: Venice* (2000)
    Well-illustrated survey useful as a visual reference for the built environment of Venice and Murano.

---

## Notes on Source Use

- **For political context**, Lane and Norwich provide the narrative backbone. Sanudo's *Diarii* is the goldmine for specific daily details.
- **For glassmaking**, McCray is the essential English source; Zecchin is the definitive Italian source for archival detail.
- **For Jewish life**, Ravid's essays and the Davis/Ravid collection are indispensable. Calimani provides readable narrative.
- **For optics**, Lindberg and Ilardi together cover the theoretical and practical dimensions. Bacon's *Opus Majus* is the key primary source for weapon speculation.
- **For daily life and sensory detail**, Fortini Brown, Muir, Burke, and Molmenti together provide a rich picture. Casola and Commynes are invaluable eyewitness accounts from the exact period.
- **For the Italian Wars**, Mallett and Shaw's 2012 book is the most current and comprehensive treatment.
